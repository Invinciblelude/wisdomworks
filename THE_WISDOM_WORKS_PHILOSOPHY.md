## Common Scenarios: How to Handle Real Situations

### Scenario 1: "I Don't Have $2,500 Right Now"

**Response**:
> "That's fine. The $2,500 is spread over 4 years. You don't pay it all upfront. Here's what we do: You pay a $500 enrollment fee to get started. That covers your first quarter of training and your HIS application.
>
> Once you close your first contract as an HIS, you'll earn that back. The rest of the tuition gets paid as you earn commissions. If you work hard, you'll never feel the cost because you're earning while you learn."

**Alternative (If they're really broke)**:
> "Tell you what. You pay the $500 enrollment fee, and I'll give you a list of 10 handyman jobs you can do this month to earn it back. Work under the $1,000 limit, stay legal, and use that money to fund your education. Sound fair?"

### Scenario 2: "How Do I Know You'll Actually Sign My Experience?"

**Response**:
> "Great question. That's exactly what makes Wisdom Works different from just working for a contractor who 'promises' to sign your affidavit.
>
> Here's my guarantee: If you complete all 4 years, submit your monthly work logs on time, pass your milestone reviews, and uphold the Honor Code, I will sign your CSLB experience affidavit. Period.
>
> In fact, it's in your enrollment agreement. If I don't sign it after you complete the program, you get a full refund. I'm legally obligated.
>
> Why would I do this? Because my reputation depends on graduating licensed contractors. If you succeed, I succeed. We're aligned."

### Scenario 3: A Student Isn't Working Out (Violations, Poor Performance)

#### Minor Issues (Late work logs, missed training):
**Approach**: Coaching conversation
> "Hey, I noticed you've missed the last two work log submissions. What's going on? You need to stay on top of this because the CSLB will audit these logs in 4 years. If there are gaps, they'll reject your application. Let's get you back on track."

**Action**:
- Set up weekly check-ins for 30 days
- Pair them with a more senior student
- Adjust their workload if they're overwhelmed

#### Major Issues (Safety violations, poor client interactions):
**Approach**: Formal warning
> "We need to have a serious conversation. [Explain the violation]. This violates our Honor Code, and it puts the school's reputation at risk. If this happens again, you'll be placed on probation. If it happens a third time, you'll be dismissed from the program and your HIS registration will be cancelled. Do you understand?"

**Action**:
- Written warning on file
- 30-day probation period
- Mandatory remedial training

#### Critical Issues (Fraud, theft, dangerous behavior):
**Approach**: Immediate dismissal
> "I have to let you go. [Explain the violation]. This is a violation of our Honor Code and it's grounds for immediate dismissal. Your HIS registration will be cancelled today, and you will not receive a CSLB experience affidavit from Wisdom Works. I'm sorry it didn't work out."

**Action**:
- Document everything
- Cancel HIS registration with CSLB
- Refund any prepaid tuition (pro-rated)
- Remove from all projects immediately

**Why This Matters**:
Your reputation is your most valuable asset. One bad student can cost you referrals, reviews, and future enrollments. It's better to cut ties early than let a problem student poison your school's brand.

### Scenario 4: "Can I Do This Part-Time? I Have Another Job"

**Response**:
> "Yes and no. The CSLB requires 4 years of 'full-time' experience, which means about 160 hours per month (40 hours/week). If you're working another job, you'll need to commit at least 20-25 hours per week to Wisdom Works projects.
>
> A lot of students do this in the beginning. They keep their day job and do handyman work on weekends or evenings until their HIS commissions start covering their bills. Then they transition to full-time.
>
> But let me be clear: If you're only giving me 5-10 hours a week, your 4-year timeline becomes a 6-8 year timeline. The CSLB counts hours, not calendar time.
>
> Can you commit to at least 20 hours per week?"

### Scenario 5: "I Failed the CSLB Exam. What Now?"

**Response**:
> "First, don't panic. About 30% of people fail the first time. Here's what we do:
>
> 1. **Review your exam results**: The CSLB tells you which sections you struggled with. We'll focus your re-study on those areas.
> 2. **Retake prep course**: I'm giving you free access to our Year 4 exam prep for another 90 days.
> 3. **Practice exams**: You need to be scoring 80%+ on practice tests before you retake the real thing.
> 4. **Retake when ready**: No rush. Take the exam again when you're confident.
>
> You've already done the hard part‚Äîthe 4 years of experience. The exam is just a test. You'll pass it."

### Scenario 6: "A Client Is Asking for Work That Requires Pulling a Permit. Can I Do It as a Handyman?"

**Response**:
> "No. Absolutely not. If a project requires a building permit, you cannot do it under the handyman exemption. Period.
>
> Here's what you do instead: Refer the client to me (the school). I'll handle the permit-required work under my license, and you'll work as my assistant on that job. You'll still log the hours, but it goes through the school's license.
>
> Never, ever tell a client to 'skip the permit.' That's how you lose your chance at a license before you even have one."

### Scenario 7: "Can I Use My Wisdom Works Training to Work for Another Contractor?"

**Response**:
> "Absolutely. In fact, I encourage it. If you get an opportunity to work for another GC and gain additional experience, take it. Just make sure:
>
> 1. You're still submitting your monthly work logs to Wisdom Works
> 2. You're documenting those hours for your CSLB application
> 3. You're not violating any non-compete (you don't have one with me)
> 4. You're upholding the Honor Code wherever you work
>
> At the end of the 4 years, if you want me to sign your affidavit, I need to have seen consistent work logs and milestone check-ins. If you disappear for 2 years, I can't vouch for your experience."
